Who Is Asker Jeukendrup?

Asker Jeukendrup, PhD, is one of the most influential sport nutrition researchers in endurance sport. His research fundamentally reshaped how athletes understand carbohydrate fueling, intestinal absorption, and gastrointestinal limitations during prolonged exercise.

Over several decades, his work bridged laboratory physiology and real-world endurance performance, influencing recommendations now used across cycling, running, triathlon, and ultra-endurance disciplines.

The Modern Fueling Framework

Traditional endurance nutrition focused primarily on glycogen depletion and post-exercise replenishment. Jeukendrup‚Äôs work contributed to a shift toward understanding fueling as a *dynamic, during-exercise process*.

This framework recognizes that performance limitations are often imposed not by muscle capacity, but by fuel delivery systems ‚Äî particularly the gastrointestinal tract.

Role of Carbohydrates in Endurance Physiology

Carbohydrates play a central role in endurance performance because they provide a rapidly oxidizable fuel source capable of supporting moderate to high exercise intensities. Muscle glycogen and blood glucose supply ATP at rates that fat oxidation alone cannot match.

From a physiological perspective, carbohydrate availability influences: motor unit recruitment, exercise economy, and the regulation of exercise intensity.

üß† Even in well-trained, fat-adapted athletes, carbohydrate oxidation remains essential when intensity increases or fatigue accumulates.

Importantly, carbohydrates also support immune function and recovery, linking performance nutrition to broader health outcomes.

Limits of Carbohydrate Oxidation

Early research demonstrated that ingesting glucose during exercise leads to a plateau in exogenous carbohydrate oxidation at approximately 60 grams per hour.

This limitation is not caused by muscular inability to use carbohydrates, but by intestinal transport constraints. Glucose absorption relies primarily on the SGLT1 transporter, which becomes saturated at moderate intake levels.

üß™ This insight shifted attention away from muscles toward the gut as a performance-limiting system.

Understanding this bottleneck was foundational to redefining endurance fueling strategies.

Multiple Transportable Carbohydrates

Jeukendrup‚Äôs landmark contribution was demonstrating that combining carbohydrates using different intestinal transporters (notably glucose and fructose) increases total absorption and oxidation rates.

Glucose and maltodextrin use the SGLT1 transporter, while fructose uses GLUT5. By engaging multiple pathways, total carbohydrate delivery to working muscles increases.

üß† This discovery enabled practical fueling targets of 90 g/h and beyond in elite endurance contexts.

This concept now underpins modern sports nutrition products and race fueling strategies across endurance disciplines.

Gut Physiology & Training the Intestine

Gastrointestinal distress is a leading cause of performance failure in endurance events. Historically, this was attributed to individual intolerance. Jeukendrup‚Äôs work reframed the gut as a trainable organ.

Regular exposure to carbohydrates during training increases transporter expression, improves gastric emptying, and enhances intestinal absorption efficiency.

üß† The gut adapts to what it is repeatedly asked to process.

This concept of ‚Äúgut training‚Äù emphasizes progressive fueling practice rather than avoidance, aligning physiological adaptation with performance goals.

Central Nervous System Effects

Beyond substrate supply to muscles, carbohydrate intake influences central nervous system function. Carbohydrate presence in the mouth and gut activates neural pathways linked to motivation and perceived effort.

These central effects help explain why carbohydrate intake can improve performance even when muscle glycogen is not fully depleted.

üß™ CNS-mediated effects reinforce the importance of carbohydrate availability beyond simple energy provision.

Integration Into Training

A critical implication of Jeukendrup‚Äôs work is that fueling strategies must be practiced during training, not improvised on race day.

Endurance athletes should align carbohydrate intake with session demands, progressively increasing both quantity and complexity as training load rises.

Performance vs Long-Term Health

Adequate carbohydrate intake supports not only performance, but also hormonal regulation, immune function, and overall energy availability.

Chronic carbohydrate restriction in high-volume endurance training increases the risk of low energy availability, endocrine disruption, and injury.

üß† Fueling is a health behavior, not just a race strategy.

Final Synthesis

The work associated with Asker Jeukendrup reshaped endurance nutrition by identifying the gut ‚Äî not the muscle ‚Äî as a primary limiter of fuel delivery.

Modern endurance fueling strategies are grounded in physiology, supported by decades of research, and essential for both performance and health.
